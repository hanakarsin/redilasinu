Working from home reduced office occupancy in Nairobi by 47% in 2020, report

A freshly published report has revealed that prime commercial office rents in Nairobi decreased from KSh 142.28 per square foot (sq. ft) per month to KSh 122.58 in the second half of 2020.

The Kenya Market Update - 2nd Half 2020 report released on Wednesday, February 9, by commercial property consultancy firm Knight Frank also showed that absorption of Grade A and B office space decreased by 50% in the period under review compared to a similar period in 2019.

This pushed the overall absorption of office space in the capital city for the year 2020 to drop to 47%.

Knight Frank Kenya Managing Director Ben Woodhams attributed the low uptake of office space to an oversupply of units and the COVID-19 pandemic, which reduced demand for offices after companies adopted remote working.

"The decline in office uptake and rental values is mainly attributed to the continued oversupply of commercial space in some locations, the ongoing economic climate, and tightened restrictions implemented in the second quarter of 2020, which affected business operations and resulted in the majority of occupiers halting their space requirements due to most of their staff working remotely," said Woodhams.

However, absorption of Grade A and B office space increased by 13% in the second half of 2020 compared to the first half of 2020.

This, according to Knight Frank, was mainly due to the lifting of lockdown measures and the gradual re-opening of businesses allowing international and local occupiers to proceed with critical business decisions and finalise transactions.

"This trend is expected to continue in the first half of 2021 from both local and international tenants as the economy fully reopens, the COVID-19 vaccine is rolled out and the expected rebound of the Kenyan economy," the report read in part.

Residential sector

The residential sector remained a buyers’ market in 2020, as prime residential rents declined sharply by 10.25%, compared to 2.8% in 2019.

This was attributed to the continued oversupply of rental properties, less disposable income due to the pandemic's unfavourable economic climate, budget cuts from multinationals, and fewer expatriates in the country.

Occupancy rates across commercial buildings

Average occupancy rates across commercial buildings at the end of the year (2020) were approximately 72%.

Landlords were open to negotiations and offering concessions such as free fit-out periods and freezing of escalations to retain and attract new tenants due to the current economic climate and increased competition.

Retail sector

Like the first half of 2020, the retail sector remained one of the hardest-hit due to the pandemic.

Though retail occupancy declined, prime retail rents remained unchanged over the review period at KSh 459.69 per square foot (sq. ft) per month due to retailers upgrading to spaces previously taken up by struggling businesses.

In a separate report, TUKO.co.ke reported that Knight Frank projected that at least 77% of Kenyans would most likely work from home in future.
